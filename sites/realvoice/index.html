        IF YOU ARE NOT SAYING WHAT YOU WANT TO SAY, WHO WILL?

        from Ana Norambuena

        Since the beginning of our life we experience the urge of communicating. First our messages are mostly encoded in crying, then we babble and after a while we develop the ability of speaking. Speech itself is an intricate dance between different parts of the body all working together for that urge in you to be expressed. The voice becomes the bridge between our inner world and the outside, a vessel through which our inner selves are carried into the world.

        Modern culture has taught us to speak primarily from the mind and to control our words with precision, to filter what we say, when we say it, and how we say it. From a young age, we learn to speak in ways that align with the expectations of parents, teachers, bosses, and colleagues. We are trained to say what others want to hear — to avoid conflict, to maintain harmony, to fit in.

        After years of training of having said what others wanted to hear, it takes courage to say what you want to say. What you really want to say. It takes time and a different training that the one offered in school.

        Reclaiming your own voice is not just about speaking freely; it’s about liberating your self from the confines of a system that has long sought to silence your heart, your archetypal lineage, your bright principles.

        Part of reclaiming the own authority out of the patriarchal system is to liberate the own voice. The liberation comes when we no longer ask for permission to speak but instead, speak. When instead of blocking your voice with emotions and thoughts you lean into the fear and speak. I am not talking about the ability to fill the air with words, which it is a survival strategy of neurotically saying lots and simultaneously, nothing. It’s not about creating noise.

        Maybe the strategy you have developed to survive has successfully deleted your capacity to speak and instead you are drowning into silence. Or perhaps you have developed a sophisticated strategy of adapting to societal conventions, allowing your true words to be camouflaged by what you know is expected, all in an effort to avoid conflict, rejection or making mistakes.

        You have learned to speak in ways that protect you, hiding your authentic self to ensure that your head does not get chopped off or you get cast out. Or you learned that the safest way is to copy someone else’s voice.

        Your voice can become a weapon, your words bullets, and you can easily kill the person in whose ears you were whispering the most beautiful love words before.

        Your voice can become magic, shaping reality and transforming energy, words cast spells.

        Using your voice consciously is deeply connected to learning to speak. Learn to form the words that follow the experience, learn to invoke the picture out of the canvas before the brush touches it. Learn to let other forces speak through you.

        Your voice can transport the deepest intimate part of you, can challenge, can inspire, can create and destroy. And your voice can reveal what a space requires—what is needed in a given moment, what direction to take. Your words can become a beacon, offering clarity, can open doors to possibility, spark creativity, and ignite a collective vision, it can become Gaia’s voice taking a stand for something bigger than yourself.

        Your voice never really disappears. Even if you don’t speak up, it cannot be silenced. It waits to unfold under all the layers of conditioning.

        If you’re ready to reclaim your voice, to learn how to speak from the heart, there are healing and training spaces for you to begin, for example, Heart Gym, Possibility Tea, Expand The Box training, and Emotional Healing Process Dojos.

        WHY I AM LEARNING TO ROAR BY MILLIE HAUGHEY

        https://medium.com/@milliehaughey/why-i-am-learning-to-roar-abade68bd40f

        I went slowly.

         

        At first, no noise came out of me. I did not tell anyone in my immediate life what I was doing — only the people holding the space for me to practice knew. It was my own quiet exploration. All I did was practice rising the energy I labelled as anger in my body and let it circulate with less judgment and more curiosity.

         

        I did my first loud, messy expression of anger out on an isolated stretch of beach one stormy evening. The first sensation was a cold fear, so I watched the ocean, letting its force guide me to my own internal oceanic place.

         

        I asked for more from myself, gently opening up, pushing deeper, digging around, moving sounds up and out of me from my belly, letting go of preconceived ideas of how it ‘should go’.

         

        Slowly I found my roar. I snarled and yelled, coughed and spluttered for almost forty-five minutes until my voice rasped. It felt fucking great.

         

        I remember showing up to work the next day and relating to people differently. It felt like I had discovered a spine I did not know I had, a voice that was foreign to me and a dawning realization that I had entered a new terrain in which to play.

         

        I found that I must practice to recondition the movement of anger energy through my body. Over the course of the next few months I took almost weekly trips out to that lonely stretch of beach... just to roar, just to yell, just to feel my anger without reason or story.

         

        This practice opened up my creative life like nothing before. I began writing daily on a blog, drawing and sleeping much better. I had more stamina and could get more things done during the day.

         

        Most importantly for me though was the unleashing of an ability to take action before I even knew why or how. This served me tremendously at work where I was a theatre nurse assisting with surgeries involving many instruments — I found an almost uncanny force at play while the tools flowed through my hands to the surgeons and I could hold a clarity of presence throughout the operation.

      • Talker's Block

        from Seth Godin

        Not your metaphorical voice. I mean your actual voice.

        It’s pretty clear to me that our speaking voice is not the result of the inevitable physical evolution of our vocal cords. It’s something our brain figured out how to do with the part of our body that keeps us alive by breathing.

        And because it’s a late addition, there are a bunch of kinks in the system.

        Talking while breathing is the beginning of the challenge.

        But it’s also worth noting that the entire process is in the middle of a huge number of sensitive muscles and nerves. This means that when we’re talking (calling attention to ourselves) we’re also trying to keep all of that stress at bay. Add microphones, Zoom, and the high-stakes world of being seen, and you can start to understand why it’s so easy to get hoarse, to sound like someone you’re not, to develop tics, to amplify your stress, and a whole host of other challenges.

        If you don’t sound like you, it might simply be because your brain is sabotaging the thing you’re trying to say. I used to riff about “no one gets talker’s block” but now I’m not so sure. I think most of us do.

        If you’ve experienced any of this, I encourage you to find a good voice coach. Not because you’re some fancy keynote speaker about to go on the TED stage. Simply because you have something to say and it would be nice to be able to say it without pain. It’s easier than ever to have a few sessions remotely, and many people I know have found it life-changing. You can find someone nearby or even watch some videos to get started.

        LEARN TO MAKE SOUND AGAIN... FOR NO REASON

        Matrix Code REALVOIC.01

        This experiment is best done in a group - like a Possibility Team with a spaceholder who 'runs' the space.

         

        1st part. The group (as well as the spaceholder) walks in the room and when meeting someone, start a conversation in gibberish. It is a regular conversation, but it totally incomprehensible. When the conversation obviously comes to an end, keep walking about the room and start another conversation in gibberish and so on for about 5-6 min.

         

        2nd part. The group forms a circle.

        The spaceholder asks for one volunteer to rotate through these three challenges:

        1. "Give me a sound for this..." (Not naming the thing. Just pointing at it, like: the floor, a person, a piano, or a refrigerator.)
        2. "Give me a sound for... dust." (Naming the thing, like: church, whale, smile, or lightening.)
        3. "Give me a sound for the opposite of... crystal." (Requesting a sound for the opposite of the thing you name, like: starfish, police, valley, or green.)

        Spaceholders, let your own imagination cut loose.

        The person might stall.

        This is because we have learn to have to learn the right answer in school. And because there is no right answer here, the person does not know what is appropriate to say.

        The spaceholder should encourage them: "Just let the first sound that comes out." or "Just open your mouth and let something come out".

        If it is clear that the sound the person makes is not the first one that was coming out, the spaceholder stops and asks: "What was the first sound that wanted to come out?"

        Then move on to the next person and the next.

        Spaceholder, to bypass the mind trying to give the right answer. You have to ask the question faster than usual and in nonlinear way (not following the circle, etc..)
